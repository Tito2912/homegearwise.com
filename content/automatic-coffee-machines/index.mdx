		          <h2 id="choose-title">Choose in 5 minutes</h2>
		          <div class="card">
            <p>
              Before comparing brands, decide which <strong>daily routine</strong> you actually want. Most buyer’s remorse comes
              from choosing the wrong workflow, not from tiny spec differences.
            </p>
            <ol>
              <li>
                <strong>What do you drink?</strong> Espresso/americano only is simpler. If you drink milk drinks daily, your milk
                system choice matters as much as the machine.
              </li>
              <li>
                <strong>How hands-on are you?</strong> Bean-to-cup machines trade control for convenience. Espresso workflows give
                more control but require dosing, tamping, and cleaning.
              </li>
              <li>
                <strong>How much cleaning will you really do?</strong> If you won’t rinse milk parts after use, choose the
                simplest milk design you can tolerate (or skip automatic milk entirely).
              </li>
              <li>
                <strong>Counter space + noise tolerance?</strong> Measure the space, check clearance for hoppers and water tanks,
                and assume grinders are audible—especially in open-plan homes.
              </li>
              <li>
                <strong>Water hardness?</strong> Hard water increases scale and bitter flavors. Plan for a filter and periodic
                descaling.
              </li>
              <li>
                <strong>Total cost (not just sticker price)?</strong> Budget for beans, filters, and cleaning/descaling products.
              </li>
            </ol>
            <p>
              If you want the simplest path: pick a bean-to-cup machine, buy fresh medium-roast beans, test your water hardness,
              and commit to a 2-minute cleaning habit.
            </p>
          </div>
	            </section>

	        <section class="stack" id="types" aria-labelledby="types-title">
	          <h2 id="types-title">Machine types (and tradeoffs)</h2>
          <div class="stack">
            <div class="card">
              <h3>Bean-to-cup super-automatic</h3>
              <p>Press a button, the machine grinds, brews, and often froths milk.</p>
              <ul>
                <li><strong>Pros:</strong> fast, consistent, low effort, great for multiple users.</li>
                <li><strong>Cons:</strong> less control, taste can be “espresso-style” rather than café-level espresso.</li>
                <li><strong>Best for:</strong> people who want repeatable drinks and a simple routine.</li>
              </ul>
            </div>
            <div class="card">
              <h3>Espresso workflow + built-in grinder</h3>
              <p>More like a café workflow: grind, dose, brew, steam (and clean) with more control.</p>
              <ul>
                <li><strong>Pros:</strong> more dialing-in and texture control, can reward good beans.</li>
                <li><strong>Cons:</strong> more steps, more cleanup, results depend on technique.</li>
                <li><strong>Best for:</strong> people who enjoy making coffee and want to tweak taste.</li>
              </ul>
            </div>
          </div>
          <div class="card">
            <h3>Where capsules/pods fit</h3>
            <p>
              Pods are the simplest to clean, but cost per cup is higher and flexibility is lower. If you want “push button,
              drink, rinse, done,” pods can be a valid choice—just not the cheapest long-term.
            </p>
          </div>
        </section>

        <section class="stack" id="milk" aria-labelledby="milk-title">
          <h2 id="milk-title">Milk systems (and cleaning)</h2>
          <div class="card">
            <p>
              Milk drinks are where “automatic” can save the most time—but only if the cleaning is realistic for you. Dried
              milk is the fastest way to get clogs and odors.
            </p>
            <h3>Three common milk workflows</h3>
            <ul>
              <li>
                <strong>Steam wand (manual):</strong> best texture potential, but you must purge/clean the wand and learn timing.
              </li>
              <li>
                <strong>Automatic frother (tube or attachment):</strong> consistent foam with less technique; requires rinsing
                and regular disassembly.
              </li>
              <li>
                <strong>Milk carafe/container system:</strong> convenient for frequent milk drinks; more parts to clean, but some
                designs are easier than others.
              </li>
            </ul>
            <h3>Practical tips that improve results</h3>
            <ul>
              <li>Use cold milk straight from the fridge; foam quality drops as milk warms.</li>
              <li>Rinse milk parts immediately after use (even 30 seconds helps).</li>
              <li>Don’t store milk residue inside tubes or carafes—clean and dry to prevent odors.</li>
              <li>If you rarely drink milk drinks, a simpler workflow may be the smarter buy.</li>
            </ul>
          </div>
        </section>

        <section class="stack" id="maintenance" aria-labelledby="maintenance-title">
          <h2 id="maintenance-title">Cleaning &amp; maintenance routine (keep it simple)</h2>
          <div class="stack">
            <div class="card">
              <h3>Daily (2–4 minutes)</h3>
              <ul>
                <li>Empty and rinse the drip tray and grounds container.</li>
                <li>Wipe the drip area and the spouts.</li>
                <li>Rinse milk parts if you made milk drinks.</li>
                <li>Refill and rinse the water tank if water has been sitting.</li>
              </ul>
              <p class="muted">This is the habit that prevents most smells and “mysterious” clogs.</p>
            </div>
            <div class="card">
              <h3>Weekly / monthly (set a reminder)</h3>
              <ul>
                <li>Deep-clean milk components (disassemble, rinse, dry).</li>
                <li>Clean the brew area and check for coffee buildup.</li>
                <li>Run the manufacturer cleaning program if supported (tablets when prompted).</li>
                <li>Check bean hopper for oily residue; wipe it dry (never wet-clean grinders).</li>
              </ul>
              <p class="muted">
                If your machine has a removable brew group, routine rinsing (per manual) is often the best reliability booster.
              </p>
            </div>
          </div>
          <div class="card">
            <h3>What to keep on hand</h3>
            <ul>
              <li>Microfiber cloth + small brush (for nooks and spouts)</li>
              <li>Descaling solution compatible with your machine</li>
              <li>Cleaning tablets (if your machine uses them)</li>
              <li>Spare water filter (if you use filters)</li>
            </ul>
          </div>
        </section>

	        <section class="stack" id="water" aria-labelledby="water-title">
	          <h2 id="water-title">Water, filters &amp; descaling (the unglamorous key)</h2>
	          <div class="card">
            <p>
              Water quality affects <strong>taste</strong> and <strong>reliability</strong>. Scale buildup reduces temperature
              stability and flow—and makes coffee taste harsher over time.
            </p>
            <h3>A safe, simple plan</h3>
            <ul>
              <li><strong>Test hardness</strong> (many machines include a strip) and set it in the menu.</li>
              <li><strong>Use a filter</strong> if the manufacturer recommends it—especially in hard-water areas.</li>
              <li><strong>Descale when prompted</strong> (or on schedule). Don’t postpone it for months.</li>
              <li>
                <strong>Avoid vinegar</strong> unless your manual explicitly allows it; use a proper descaler to protect seals.
              </li>
              <li><strong>Keep tanks clean</strong>: rinse the water reservoir regularly so water doesn’t go stale.</li>
            </ul>
            <p class="muted">
              If you want to optimize taste further, your biggest lever is still the basics: fresh beans, correct grind range,
              and clean internals.
	            </p>

        <section class="stack" id="faq" aria-labelledby="faq-title">
          <h2 id="faq-title">FAQ</h2>
          <div class="card">
            <details>
              <summary>Do super-automatic machines make “real espresso”?</summary>
              <div>
                <p>
                  They make espresso-style coffee with pressure and fine grind, but the taste and texture can differ from
                  café espresso. Their main advantage is repeatability and convenience.
                </p>
              </div>
            </details>
            <details>
              <summary>How often do I need to descale an automatic coffee machine?</summary>
              <div>
                <p>
                  It depends on your water hardness and usage. Test hardness, set it in the machine, and follow the machine’s
                  prompts or the manufacturer schedule.
                </p>
              </div>
            </details>
            <details>
              <summary>Which beans work best in built-in grinders?</summary>
              <div>
                <p>
                  Use fresh, non-oily beans (often medium roasts). Very dark, oily beans can leave residue and may increase
                  grinder jams and stale flavors.
                </p>
              </div>
            </details>
            <details>
              <summary>Is an automatic milk system worth it?</summary>
              <div>
                <p>
                  If you regularly drink cappuccinos or lattes, it saves time and improves consistency. The tradeoff is more
                  parts to rinse and clean to avoid dried milk and odors.
                </p>
              </div>
            </details>
            <details>
              <summary>Can I use vinegar to descale?</summary>
              <div>
                <p>
                  Avoid vinegar unless your manufacturer explicitly allows it. Use the brand-recommended descaler to protect
                  seals and avoid lingering taste.
                </p>
              </div>
            </details>
            <details>
              <summary>What’s the #1 habit that keeps these machines reliable?</summary>
              <div>
                <p>
                  Empty and rinse the drip tray and milk parts often, and don’t ignore descaling prompts. Small, regular
                  cleaning prevents most clogs and bad smells.
                </p>
              </div>
            </details>
            <details>
              <summary>LatteGo vs LatteCrema: which is easier to clean?</summary>
              <div>
                <p>
                  LatteGo (no tubes) is often faster to rinse. LatteCrema can be very convenient for milk drinks, but it
                  requires a consistent rinse/clean routine. Pick the system you’ll actually maintain.
                </p>
              </div>
            </details>
            <details>
              <summary>Should I change grind settings while the grinder is running?</summary>
              <div>
                <p>
                  Often yes: adjust grind while the grinder is running (follow your manual). Then pull 2–3 coffees to judge the
                  change before adjusting again.
                </p>
              </div>
            </details>
            <details>
              <summary>Can I use plant-based milk?</summary>
              <div>
                <p>
                  Yes, but foam quality varies by type (oat/soy often froth better than almond). Rinse immediately—plant milks
                  also dry out and create buildup.
                </p>
              </div>
            </details>
            <details>
              <summary>How do I fix coffee that tastes too bitter or too sour?</summary>
              <div>
                <p>
                  Change one variable at a time: strength first, then grind. Too sour: go slightly finer/stronger; too bitter:
                  back off gradually. Also clean—old oils create a “dirty” bitterness.
                </p>
              </div>
            </details>
            <details>
              <summary>Very hard water: filter or bottled water?</summary>
              <div>
                <p>
                  Start by testing hardness. A compatible filter can reduce scale, but you still need to descale. If your tap
                  water is very hard, lower-scale water (or a blend) can help taste and maintenance.
                </p>
              </div>
            </details>
          </div>
