    <section id="pet-messes">
      <h2>What pet messes actually are</h2>
      <p>
        Buying for pets is easier when you separate the “mess types”. Different features solve different messes.
      </p>
      <ul>
        <li><strong>Hair:</strong> wraps brushes, clogs airflow, and reduces pickup fast.</li>
        <li><strong>Tracked debris:</strong> litter, sand, kibble, tiny leaves. Collects at edges and corners.</li>
        <li><strong>Paw prints:</strong> light film that builds on hard floors, especially near doors and bowls.</li>
        <li><strong>Odors:</strong> usually a mop pad/dock hygiene issue, not a vacuum power issue.</li>
      </ul>
    </section>

    <section id="hair-brushes">
      <h2>Hair: brushes, rollers, and clogs</h2>
      <p>
        In pet homes, the robot is only as good as your brush routine. The goal is not “never clean it”. The goal is “clean
        it so fast you do not mind doing it weekly”.
      </p>

      <div className="card">
        <h3>Brush checklist (pet homes)</h3>
        <ul>
          <li>Remove the main brush without tools.</li>
          <li>Open end caps or remove wrapped hair easily.</li>
          <li>Side brush is easy to remove and replace.</li>
          <li>Replacement brushes and filters are available (and not overpriced).</li>
        </ul>
      </div>

      <p>
        If you notice weaker pickup after a few weeks, assume it is airflow and brush drag first: clean the filter, remove
        hair from the brush, then deep clean the brush housing.
      </p>
      <p className="muted">
        For a full schedule, use the <a href="/blog/how-to-clean-and-maintain-robot-vacuum/">maintenance guide</a>.
      </p>
    </section>

    <section id="litter">
      <h2>Litter, kibble, and corners</h2>
      <p>
        Most robots handle tracked litter and kibble fine, but two things matter: edge behavior and bin management. Pet
        debris tends to accumulate at walls, under cabinets, and around bowls.
      </p>
      <ul>
        <li><strong>Edges:</strong> plan a weekly manual corner pass even with a good robot.</li>
        <li><strong>No-go zones:</strong> keep the robot away from litter mats if tracking is heavy.</li>
        <li><strong>Frequency:</strong> more frequent runs prevent “litter lines” along baseboards.</li>
      </ul>
    </section>

    <section id="mopping-odor">
      <h2>Mopping and odor control</h2>
      <p>
        Mopping is where pet homes can win big, but only if the mop stays clean and dry. A damp pad left overnight is a
        smell generator.
      </p>
      <div className="card">
        <h3>Odor control rules</h3>
        <ul>
          <li>Rinse pads the same day (or use a dock that washes pads).</li>
          <li>Empty dirty water and wipe trays regularly (if your dock uses water).</li>
          <li>Do not add random floor cleaners unless the manual says it is approved.</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
      <p>
        If mopping is a priority, the dock workflow (wash and dry) can matter more than anything else. Compare dock
        capabilities in the <a href="/robot-vacuum-mop-comparison/">comparison chart</a>.
      </p>
    </section>

    <section id="obstacles">
      <h2>Toys, cords, accidents: obstacle reality</h2>
      <p>
        Obstacle avoidance is improving, but pets and kids create exactly the kind of chaos robots hate: small objects,
        cords, and surprises.
      </p>
      <ul>
        <li><strong>Prep pass:</strong> pick up strings, socks, toys, and fragile items.</li>
        <li><strong>No-go zones:</strong> block areas you cannot keep tidy (toy corners, feeding stations).</li>
        <li><strong>Accidents:</strong> do not rely on a robot to detect or avoid pet accidents 100% of the time.</li>
      </ul>
    </section>

    <section id="allergies">
      <h2>Allergies and dust exposure</h2>
      <p>
        If you are sensitive to dust and dander, your goal is to reduce dust exposure during emptying and maintenance.
      </p>
      <ul>
        <li><strong>Auto-empty docks:</strong> fewer manual bin dumps and less dust in the air.</li>
        <li><strong>Filter care:</strong> follow the manual (some filters are not washable).</li>
        <li><strong>Maintenance routine:</strong> do a fast weekly clean so you never face a “gross” deep clean.</li>
      </ul>
    </section>

    <section id="routine">
      <h2>A pet-home routine that actually sticks</h2>
      <p>
        The best routine is the one you will run. Here is a simple baseline that works for most pet homes.
      </p>
      <div className="card">
        <ul>
          <li><strong>Daily:</strong> short vacuum run in high-traffic rooms (kitchen, entry, living).</li>
          <li><strong>2-4x/week:</strong> mop hard-floor rooms if paw prints build up.</li>
          <li><strong>Weekly:</strong> remove hair from brushes, clean filter, wipe sensors.</li>
          <li><strong>Monthly:</strong> deep clean wheels, brush housing, and dock trays/tanks.</li>
        </ul>
