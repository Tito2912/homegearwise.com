    <section id="home-fit">
      <h2>Step 1: match the robot to your home</h2>
      <p>
        A robot vacuum is not a single product category. It is a routine machine. The right model is the one that can run
        reliably in your space without constant babysitting.
      </p>

      <h3>Hard floors</h3>
      <ul>
        <li><strong>Main goal:</strong> frequent light cleaning (dust, crumbs, footprints).</li>
        <li><strong>What to prioritize:</strong> stable mapping, good edge behavior, and a mop routine you will maintain.</li>
        <li><strong>Common mistake:</strong> paying extra for extreme suction when the bigger constraint is mopping hygiene.</li>
      </ul>

      <h3>Carpet-heavy homes</h3>
      <ul>
        <li><strong>Main goal:</strong> consistent pickup without hair clogs.</li>
        <li><strong>What to prioritize:</strong> brush cleaning access, filter care, and a schedule you can run often.</li>
        <li><strong>Common mistake:</strong> ignoring consumables (filters/brushes) then losing performance quickly.</li>
      </ul>

      <h3>Mixed floors (hard floors + rugs)</h3>
      <ul>
        <li><strong>Main goal:</strong> reliable room-based routines (vacuum here, mop there, avoid this rug).</li>
        <li><strong>What to prioritize:</strong> no-go zones, multi-room control, and a clear rug strategy in mop modes.</li>
        <li><strong>Common mistake:</strong> choosing a mop combo without thinking about how it keeps rugs dry.</li>
      </ul>

      <div className="card">
        <strong>Fast home-fit rule</strong>
        <ul>
          <li>If your home is cluttered: prioritize no-go zones and obstacle handling.</li>
          <li>If you have pets: prioritize brush access, filtration, and dock hygiene.</li>
          <li>If you mop often: prioritize pad washing and pad drying workflows.</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
    </section>

    <section id="mopping-reality">
      <h2>Step 2: set realistic mopping expectations</h2>
      <p>
        Most vacuum + mop robots are designed for <strong>maintenance mopping</strong>: dust, footprints, and light spills.
        If you buy expecting “deep mop results” without manual help, you will be disappointed.
      </p>

      <h3>Three mopping tiers (what people actually mean)</h3>
      <ol>
        <li>
          <strong>Wipe pass:</strong> light pad drag for daily freshness. Best for hard floors with frequent runs.
        </li>
        <li>
          <strong>Rotating pads:</strong> better agitation for footprints and light sticky spots, still not a replacement for
          a full mop.
        </li>
        <li>
          <strong>Dock-supported mopping:</strong> when pads are washed and dried automatically, mopping becomes practical
          to run often (and odor stays under control).
        </li>
      </ol>

      <h3>Rugs and carpets: how the robot keeps them dry</h3>
      <ul>
        <li><strong>Avoidance:</strong> the robot avoids rugs when in mop mode. Simple, but depends on map accuracy.</li>
        <li><strong>Mop lifting:</strong> the robot lifts the mop when it detects rugs. Helpful in mixed rooms.</li>
        <li><strong>Room-based routines:</strong> vacuum rugs first, then return to mop hard floors only.</li>
      </ul>

      <p>
        If you are shopping primarily for mopping, read the mopping and dock sections in our
        <a href="/robot-vacuum-mop-comparison/">comparison chart</a> and prioritize hygiene (wash/dry) over hype.
      </p>
    </section>

    <section id="docks">
      <h2>Step 3: pick the right dock automation</h2>
      <p>
        The dock is where “hands-off” lives or dies. The question is not whether the dock is fancy. The question is:
        <strong>how much handling do you want to do every week?</strong>
      </p>

      <div className="card">
        <h3>What automation actually reduces work</h3>
        <ul>
          <li><strong>Auto-emptying:</strong> fewer bin dumps, less dust exposure.</li>
          <li><strong>Pad washing:</strong> makes frequent mopping realistic.</li>
          <li><strong>Pad drying:</strong> reduces odor and “wet funk” if you mop often.</li>
        </ul>
      </div>

      <h3>What still needs you (even with premium docks)</h3>
      <ul>
        <li>Brush hair removal and filter care (weekly).</li>
        <li>Wipe sensors and charging contacts (weekly).</li>
        <li>Dock tray cleaning and tank hygiene (regular).</li>
        <li>Consumables replacement (pads, filters, bags if used).</li>
      </ul>

      <p className="muted">
        If you want a dock-forward pick, see the iRobot model section in the <a href="/robot-vacuum-mop-comparison/">comparison chart</a>.

    <section id="hair">
      <h2>Step 4: hair, brushes, and filters</h2>
      <p>
        Hair is the #1 real-world performance killer. The best robot is the one you can maintain quickly. If you dread
        cleaning it, it will stop working well.
      </p>

      <h3>Brush access checklist</h3>
      <ul>
        <li>Can you remove the main brush without tools?</li>
        <li>Can you open end caps to remove wrapped hair?</li>
        <li>Are replacement brushes and filters easy to find?</li>
        <li>Is the filter washable or tap-only (check the manual)?</li>
      </ul>

    <section id="navigation">
      <h2>Step 5: navigation, no-go zones, obstacles</h2>
      <p>
        In 2026, navigation is not only “mapping”. It is consistency: does the robot run reliably, avoid the problems you
        care about, and keep your room setup stable across updates?
      </p>

      <div className="card">
        <h3>No-go zones are a buying feature</h3>
        <ul>
          <li>Block pet bowls and litter areas.</li>
          <li>Block cords, fragile decor, and floor vents.</li>
          <li>Block “always messy” zones (kids play area) unless you prep first.</li>
        </ul>
      </div>

      <h3>Obstacle avoidance reality check</h3>
      <ul>
        <li>Robots still struggle with thin cords, socks, and small toys.</li>
        <li>Pet accidents require manual pickup first. No robot guarantees safe handling.</li>
        <li>A quick prep pass is normal. Plan it into the routine.</li>
      </ul>
    </section>

    <section id="maintenance-cost">
      <h2>Maintenance cost (the hidden budget)</h2>
      <p>
        The sticker price is not the total cost. Filters, brushes, pads, and sometimes bags are the long-term reality. The
        right way to think about it: <strong>your maintenance time</strong> plus <strong>your consumables cost</strong>.
      </p>

      <div className="card">
        <h3>Maintenance cadence (baseline)</h3>
        <ul>
          <li><strong>Weekly:</strong> remove hair from brushes, clean filter, wipe sensors.</li>
          <li><strong>Monthly:</strong> deep clean wheels/brush housing, rinse non-electronic parts, clean dock tray/tanks.</li>
          <li><strong>As needed:</strong> replace filters/brushes/pads (follow your manual and usage).</li>
        </ul>
      </div>

    <section id="avoid">
      <h2>What to ignore in marketing</h2>
      <p>
        Many listings are optimized for clicks, not clarity. Here is what tends to mislead buyers.
      </p>
      <ul>
        <li>
          <strong>Extreme suction multipliers:</strong> they rarely predict your outcome as well as brush design and
          maintenance.
        </li>
        <li>
          <strong>“Mops like a human” claims:</strong> most robots do maintenance mopping. Expect improvement, not magic.
        </li>
        <li>
          <strong>Gigantic map counts:</strong> most people need stable room names and zones more than many maps.
        </li>
        <li>
          <strong>Bundles:</strong> accessories can differ by region and bundle. Always check what is in the box.
        </li>
      </ul>
    </section>

    <section id="first-week">
      <h2>Validate your choice (first-week test plan)</h2>
      <p>
        A robot should earn its spot. Use the first week to validate mapping, dock workflow, and whether maintenance is
        realistic for you.
      </p>
      <div className="card">
        <ol>
          <li><strong>Day 1:</strong> map the home, name rooms, add no-go zones for cords and bowls.</li>
          <li><strong>Day 2:</strong> run a full vacuum pass. Check edges and where hair collects.</li>
          <li><strong>Day 3:</strong> run mop-only in one room. Check how it handles rugs and transitions.</li>
          <li><strong>Day 4:</strong> test the dock workflow (emptying, washing, drying) and note the noise.</li>
          <li><strong>Day 5:</strong> do a 5-minute maintenance cycle. If it feels painful, it will not last long-term.</li>
          <li><strong>Day 6-7:</strong> schedule the routine you actually want (time of day, room order, frequency).</li>
        </ol>
